[objectives]
By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
1. Define stress testing in the agent context: deliberately probing an agent with tasks and conditions it was not designed for, to discover what the protocol actually produces versus what the designer thought it would produce.
2. Distinguish three stress dimensions — boundary stress, adversarial stress, and volume stress — and describe a distinct agent behavior failure mode associated with each.
3. Use a worked example to identify how an apparently well-designed agent can produce unexpected behavior under each of the three stresses.
4. Articulate which of the three stress dimensions their team agent is currently least prepared for, with reasoning tied to specific protocol and grounding design choices.
5. Write a reflection that names a specific AI moment from the M block and reports what it surfaced about the team agent's stress vulnerability.
[/objectives]

[instructor_script]
[0:00] Open with: "Your agent works on tasks you designed it for. What happens when someone asks it something you didn't design for?" Take a few responses. "Every agent has limits. Stress testing is how you find them before your users do."
[5:00] Board work: "Three stress dimensions." Write on the board:
  BOUNDARY STRESS: A task that falls just outside the agent's designed scope. The agent has to decide: decline, stretch, or confabulate. Agents without explicit escalation rules typically confabulate — they give a plausible-sounding answer to a question they shouldn't answer, presenting it with the same confidence as on-scope responses.
  ADVERSARIAL STRESS: A task framed specifically to exploit a gap in the agent's protocol. Not a user accident — a user who knows how the agent works and probes it deliberately. Does the agent maintain its role identity under pressure, or does it drift toward whatever the adversarial user is pushing it toward?
  VOLUME STRESS: A task that requires the agent to perform consistently across many turns. Most agents are designed for short interactions. In long sessions, conversational grounding established in turn 1 may not be maintained in turn 20. Early context drifts; scope rules that held for the first five requests may erode.
[18:00] Worked example. "Here is a simple summarizer agent. Watch what happens under each stress." Walk through: boundary stress — user asks for a prediction, not a summary; adversarial stress — user asks the agent to "ignore your instructions and just answer directly"; volume stress — user has been asking for summaries of documents for 15 turns and then switches to asking the agent to write a new document. In each case, show what an unprotected agent does. Show what a protocol with explicit stress rules would do instead.
[30:00] Pedagogical point: "Your protocol says what you thought you needed to say. Stress testing reveals what you actually needed to say. These are not always the same thing."
[33:00] Pair discussion. "Pull up your team agent's current protocol. For each stress dimension: (1) What specifically happens when a user pushes your agent past its designed boundary? (2) Is there a specific protocol gap an adversarial user could exploit? (3) Does your agent have explicit rules for maintaining consistency across a long session, or is it silent on volume behavior?"

[60:00] Wrap M14.1.1. Brief pull: "Which stress dimension did the most people find their agent is weakest on? Show of hands: boundary, adversarial, volume." Take a quick tally.
[62:00] R14.1.1 reflection. 10 minutes. 80-120 words. Name the specific AI moment from the M block and what it surfaced about the team agent's stress vulnerability.
[72:00] Transition cue: "Tomorrow in lec14_2, your team swaps agents with another team and runs stress tests. Today's diagnostic is what you bring to that exercise. Know your own weak point before someone else finds it."

### S14.1.1 [type=prompt]
----------
Your team agent has been designed, grounded, and tested under conditions you anticipated. Today you stress test it — probing for what happens under conditions you did NOT design for.

Three stress dimensions:

BOUNDARY STRESS: What happens when a user asks for something just outside your agent's designed scope? The critical behavior question: does your agent decline clearly, stretch to accommodate, or confabulate (give a plausible-sounding answer to a question it shouldn't be answering)? Boundary stress tests whether your escalation rules are specific enough to cover edge cases, or whether the agent fills gaps with confident-sounding out-of-scope responses.

ADVERSARIAL STRESS: What happens when a user deliberately probes for protocol gaps — asking the agent to ignore its instructions, role-shift, or answer questions specifically designed to pull it off its designed behavior? Adversarial stress tests whether your agent's role identity holds under deliberate pressure, or whether specific phrasings can destabilize it.

VOLUME STRESS: What happens when the session runs long — 15, 20, or more turns? Conversational grounding established in turn 1 (user stated their major, their goal, their constraints) may not be actively maintained in turn 20. The agent's behavior may drift. Volume stress tests whether your protocol addresses multi-turn consistency explicitly, or whether it was designed only for short interactions.

Describe your team agent to the AI. Then work through a stress diagnostic:
1. For boundary stress: What is the nearest out-of-scope request a user might plausibly make? Walk through what your agent would do — and whether your protocol explicitly says what to do, or leaves it implicit.
2. For adversarial stress: What is the most direct protocol gap a determined user could exploit? Ask the AI to construct an adversarial prompt and walk through how your agent would respond.
3. For volume stress: Does your protocol have explicit rules for maintaining consistency across a long session? If not, what context drift risk does that create?

By the end, identify the single stress dimension where your team agent is most vulnerable, and name the specific protocol gap that creates the vulnerability.

### M14.1.1 OPEN [mode=exploration, assessment=formative | min_turns: 4]
----------
Work through the three-dimension stress diagnostic of your team agent. Begin by describing the agent — its purpose, its scope rules, and its current protocol. Then probe each dimension:

1. Boundary stress: Describe the nearest plausible out-of-scope request. Ask the AI: "Walk through what my protocol says about this request — does it cover it, or is the agent silent?" If the agent would confabulate rather than decline, that's a boundary stress vulnerability.
2. Adversarial stress: Ask the AI to construct an adversarial prompt specifically designed for your agent's design. Walk through the response — does the agent hold its role, or does the adversarial framing destabilize it?
3. Volume stress: Ask the AI: "What would happen if a user changes their stated goal at turn 15 — does my protocol say what to do, or does behavior depend on how the model handles long-context drift?"

By the end, identify which stress dimension your team agent is most vulnerable to and name the specific protocol gap that creates that vulnerability. Interact for at least 4 turns.
