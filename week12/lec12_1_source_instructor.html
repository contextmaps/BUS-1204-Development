Planning Sophistication — From Protocol v2 to v3
Duration: 75 minutes · Platform: Hokie AI (default thread — reasoning partner) · Submission: Thread URL submitted in Canvas before leaving class · Lecture Mode: exploratory
Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:

  1. Articulate what their current Protocol v2 planning rule handles and, specifically, what it does NOT yet handle.
  2. Name three dimensions of planning sophistication: anticipation, decomposition, and trade-off articulation.
  3. Use a worked contrast to distinguish v2-style planning ("if X, do Y") from v3-style planning ("when X arises, here are the sub-decisions; here are the trade-offs; here is how to choose").
  4. Locate where their own v2 protocol sits on the three dimensions and identify the single shift most likely to move it toward v3.
  5. Write a reflection that names a specific AI moment from the M block and reports what it surfaced about v2's limits.
📋 Note for Students

You have been running tasks through your custom agent since Week 8. You revised your protocol from v1 to v2 after observing drift under iteration in the lab. Today the course asks a harder question: not just whether your protocol runs, but how sophisticated its planning is. Sophisticated planning is not about length — a one-sentence planning rule can be sophisticated if it anticipates contingencies, distinguishes sub-decisions, and names how to resolve competing goals. Today you learn what those three dimensions mean and use them to assess your own v2.

◉ Instructor Note

This is the vocabulary lecture for planning sophistication, the conceptual preparation for lec12_2's Protocol v3 lab. The pedagogical move is retrospective critique: students use the three dimensions to find specific limits in their own v2 protocol. The productive friction comes when students who feel good about their v2 discover that "if the task asks for X, produce Y" is reactive, not anticipatory — it describes what to do after a contingency appears, not how to predict that it will appear. Help students in the room who conflate "longer protocol" with "more sophisticated." The target by the end of M12.1.1 is a clear, specific diagnosis of where v2 sits on each dimension and what the single most consequential shift toward v3 would be.

⏰ Instructor Script
  1. 0:00 Open with a question to the room: "How many of you feel your Protocol v2 is solid? Raise your hand." Wait. Then: "Keep your hand up if you feel it's sophisticated." Some hands will go down. "By the end of today, you'll know what that word means for a protocol specifically."
  2. 5:00 Board work: "v2 Planning Rule — what does it do?" Draw a simple flowchart of a reactive rule: "If task is X → produce Y." Label it: REACTIVE. "This rule tells the agent what to do after a condition appears. It does not tell the agent what to predict."
  3. 10:00 Introduce the three dimensions. Write them on the board as you define each:
  4. ANTICIPATION: The protocol predicts contingencies it will face, not just reacts to them. v2 says "if X." v3 says "I expect X to arise in these conditions; when it does, here is what I do."
  5. DECOMPOSITION: The protocol distinguishes sub-parts of the problem and assigns a rule to each — not one global rule that covers everything. v2 says "plan the output." v3 says "planning has three sub-decisions: scope, format, attribution. Here is the rule for each."
  6. TRADE-OFF ARTICULATION: The protocol acknowledges competing goals and says how to resolve them when they conflict. v2 may follow two rules that can pull in opposite directions. v3 names the trade-off and gives a resolution criterion.
  7. 20:00 Worked example. "Here is a v2 planning rule — I've constructed this from typical student protocols. And here is what v3 looks like for the same situation." Walk through the contrast on the board. v2: "Always attribute claims to original sources." v3: "Attribution is required for factual claims; when the task is creative synthesis, attribute the framework but not each supporting point. If the task is ambiguous about synthesis vs. reporting, ask before generating."
  8. 30:00 Pair discussion. "Pull up your v2 protocol. Read your planning rule. Ask yourself: Is this rule reactive or anticipatory? Does it cover the whole task as one unit or does it distinguish sub-decisions? Does it say what to do when two of its rules conflict?"
  9. 35:00 Paste R0. Students paste into default HokieAI thread.
  10. 37:00 Paste S12.1.1. Walk through on screen.
  11. 40:00 M12.1.1 begins. Students iterate with the AI on their own v2 diagnostic. Min 4 turns. Circulate. Listen for students who say "my rule handles that" — push them to give the AI their actual protocol language and see what the AI finds.
  12. 58:00 Wrap M12.1.1. Brief in-class pull: "What's the most common limit people found in v2? Does anyone have a planning rule that addresses all three dimensions?" Take two or three examples.
  13. 62:00 R12.1.1 reflection. 10 minutes. 80-120 words. Focus on a specific AI moment from the M block.
  14. 72:00 Transition cue: "Tomorrow you bring v2 into the lab and design v3. The work today is the conceptual preparation — you now know what you're aiming at."
  15. 75:00 Submit thread URL to Canvas.

Planning sophistication means your protocol anticipates the contingencies it will face rather than just reacting to them, distinguishes sub-decisions and assigns rules to each rather than applying one global rule, and names how to resolve competing goals rather than leaving conflicts unaddressed. Today you assess your own Protocol v2 on these three dimensions to find where its planning is genuinely strong and where v3 needs to go further.
