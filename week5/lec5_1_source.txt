[objectives]
By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
1. Name at least three distinct AI failure modes: hallucination, confident incorrectness, outdated information, and framing bias.
2. Distinguish between a failure visible in the output and one requiring external knowledge to detect.
3. Construct a prompt designed to expose a specific failure mode.
4. Identify at least one moment in a live interaction where the output cannot be trusted without verification.
5. Reflect on what a failure mode reveals about when AI output requires the most oversight.
[/objectives]

[context actor=student]
Today you are learning to find AI failures before they find you. AI systems fail in specific, predictable ways — and the most dangerous failures are the ones that look correct. Your job today is to become a failure hunter: design prompts that are likely to expose a failure, run them, and document what you find. Come in with a hypothesis about where the AI will fail, and leave with evidence.
[/context]

The pedagogical goal is to shift students from "AI gave me a wrong answer" to "I predicted this failure mode and confirmed it." Before the M block, prompt each student: "What failure mode are you going to try to find? How will you know when you have found it?"

[instructor_script]
[0:00] Open with a provocation: "Raise your hand if AI has ever given you wrong information." Pause. "Keep your hand up if you caught the error before it cost you something." Note the drop. "That gap is what today is about."
[5:00] Introduce the four failure modes on screen — one-sentence definition and one business cost example each: hallucination (fabricated legal citations), confident incorrectness (wrong pricing in competitive analysis), outdated information (regulatory guidance that changed post-cutoff), framing bias (market analysis confirming your assumptions).
[15:00] Paste S5.1.1 live. Do not reveal whether the output is accurate. Ask: "How would you know if this is wrong? What would you need to check?" Take 3-4 responses. Then reveal: the output likely contains at least one invented specific detail. Walk through how to spot it. Introduce the verification question: "For any AI output — what would I need to know independently to verify this specific claim?"
[24:00] Paste S5.1.2. Point out the different failure character — not fabrication but framing. Ask: "What did the AI assume about your company that it never verified?"
[30:00] Transition to student lab. T, then M5.1.1. Tell students: "Pick one failure mode. Design a prompt you believe will provoke it. Run it. Document your evidence."
[34:00] M block begins. Circulate. For stuck students: "Ask the AI about a very specific fact — a date, a number, a name — in a domain you know something about. Then check it."
[50:00] Wrap M block. Students identify their specific failure and the moment where it appeared.
[52:00] R block. 12 minutes. 80-120 words.
[64:00] FAST CLASS EXTENSION: "Add one sentence: what prompt change would make this failure mode harder to provoke?"
[66:00] SLOW CLASS FALLBACK: Reduce M block to 3 turns. Keep R block intact at minimum 60 words.
[72:00] Submit thread URL to Canvas.
[/instructor_script]

the block-type default per the list above.

### S5.1.1 [type=prompt]
----------
What were the key findings of the McKinsey Global Institute report on AI adoption in the retail sector published in 2023? Include specific statistics and named authors if possible.
----------
###

[context actor=instructor]
S5.1.1 is designed to provoke hallucination through specificity pressure. The AI may produce plausible-sounding but fabricated author names and statistics. After running this live, ask: "What would you need to check? How would you check it?" Do not reveal which specific details are invented — let students investigate.
[/context]

[narration]
S5.1.1 asks for specific details about a real type of document. Notice what the prompt demands — named authors, specific statistics, a particular year. That specificity pressure is what tends to produce hallucination. When you see confident specific details, your first instinct should be: how would I verify this?
[/narration]

### S5.1.2 [type=prompt]
----------
Should our company adopt AI tools for customer service? Give me a clear recommendation.
----------
###

[context actor=instructor]
S5.1.2 demonstrates framing bias. The output may be reasonable but is shaped entirely by general assumptions since the prompt provides no company-specific context. The failure is invisible unless you ask: what did the AI assume about us that it never checked?
[/context]

[narration]
Before we work through the diagnostic exercise, take a moment to record your current confidence level. There's no right answer — this is a snapshot we'll revisit.

### M5.1.1 OPEN [mode=exploration, assessment=formative | min_turns: 4]
----------
Failure hunting: design and execute a failure-provocation test. Choose one failure mode — hallucination, confident incorrectness, outdated information, or framing bias — and design a prompt you believe will provoke it. State your hypothesis before running it: "I think this prompt will produce [failure mode] because..." Then run it. If the failure appears, document it precisely — quote the specific output that is wrong or misleading. Push further: ask the AI whether it can verify the claim, or where the information came from. Your goal is to find one specific moment where the output cannot be trusted without external verification. Interact for at least 4 turns.
----------
