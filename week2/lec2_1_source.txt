[objectives]
By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
1. Distinguish between AI as a collaborator and AI as a decision-maker.
2. Identify business tasks where AI adds genuine value versus tasks where it should be constrained.
3. Explain why trust in AI must be calibrated to task type and context, not assumed from output quality.
4. Construct a prompt that explicitly frames AI as a collaborator and observe how framing changes behavior.
5. Reflect on a specific moment in their own interaction where the boundary between collaboration and decision-making became visible.
[/objectives]

[context actor=student]
Today you are exploring a fundamental distinction that shapes every professional interaction with AI: the difference between using AI as a collaborator — a drafting partner, an idea generator, a pattern finder — and mistakenly treating it as a decision-maker with authority over outcomes. This is not a technical distinction. It is a responsibility distinction. You will run a live experiment in Hokie AI and observe what happens when that framing changes.
[/context]

[context actor=instructor]
This is an exploratory lecture. Students are not expected to produce polished analysis — they are expected to notice things. Your role is to narrate what is visible on screen, ask questions that slow down the observation ("what changed between those two outputs?"), and resist the urge to explain before students have had time to look. The M block is the heart of the session — do not abbreviate it. The S block sets up a contrast that the M block then exploits.
[/context]

[instructor_script]
[0:00] Open with a direct framing statement — no warm-up questions: "Last week you learned to read AI output critically. Today you will learn to shape AI behavior intentionally. The single most important habit you will develop in this course starts here." Do not ask for responses. Move directly to the conceptual framing.
[5:00] Introduce the collaborator-decision-maker spectrum verbally. Draw it on the board or project it: a line from "fully collaborative" on the left to "fully autonomous" on the right. Ask students: "Where on this line does a calculator sit? Where does Hokie AI sit?" Take 2-3 responses. Establish that Hokie AI sits closer to collaborative but can be pushed toward autonomous by how we phrase requests.
[12:00] Display S2.1.1 on screen. Walk through the prompt structure: "Notice what this prompt does not say. It does not say 'decide.' It does not say 'recommend.' It says 'help me think through.' That word choice is deliberate." Paste S2.1.1 live in Hokie AI and project the output. Do not comment on the output immediately — give students 60 seconds to read silently.
[18:00] Ask one question: "What is Hokie AI doing in this response — collaborating or deciding?" Take 3-4 responses. Then reveal the contrast: "Now watch what happens when we change one phrase." Paste the contrast version from S2.1.2 and project both outputs side by side.
[25:00] Guide a 5-minute whole-class comparison. Focus questions: "Where did authority shift?" "Which version would you be more likely to act on without checking?" "Which version puts more responsibility on you?" Do not resolve these — leave them as live questions students will carry into the M block.
[32:00] Transition to student-run phase. Display T on screen. Explain that the session opener anchors the AI's role for the session. Students paste T, wait for acknowledgment, then move to M2.1.1. Tell them: "You have 15 minutes. Your goal is not to produce something — it is to find one moment where you can see the line between collaboration and decision-making. That moment is what you will write about."
[35:00] M block begins. Circulate. Do not answer "is this right?" questions. Answer only "what did you notice?" questions. If a student seems stuck, prompt: "Try asking Hokie AI to make the decision for you instead of help you think. What changes?"
[50:00] Wrap M block. Give students 2 minutes to identify the specific moment they will write about before opening R2.1.1. Say: "You are not summarizing your conversation. You are diagnosing one thing you saw."
[52:00] R block begins. Students write in the text box and copy to Hokie AI. 12 minutes.
[64:00] FAST CLASS EXTENSION: If students finish early, ask: "Write one more sentence — what would you do differently in the M block if you ran it again?" This deepens the reflection without opening a new block.
[67:00] SLOW CLASS FALLBACK: If time is short, reduce the M block minimum from 4 turns to 3, and reduce R2.1.1 minimum from 80 words to 60. Do not cut the R block entirely — it is the assessed contribution for this lecture.
[70:00] Remind students to submit thread URL to Canvas before leaving. Do not dismiss until all submissions are confirmed.
[/instructor_script]

[narration]
Today's session is built around a single distinction that sounds simple but turns out to be surprisingly slippery in practice: the difference between asking AI to help you think, and asking AI to decide for you. You will run two prompts designed to make that difference visible, then spend time in a free exploration block finding your own example of where the line sits. The reflection at the end is short — 80 to 120 words — but it asks you to be specific. One moment from your own interaction. Not a general observation about AI.
[/narration]

BUS 1204 — Week 2 Lecture 1. You are an AI collaborator for this session, not a decision-maker. ---
**AI Interaction Protocol**

In this session, you will receive blocks of content with the
following structural pattern:

```
### <BlockID>
----------
<content>
----------
###
```

Some blocks include an interaction-mode directive on a separate
line inside the dashed envelope:

```
### <BlockID>
----------
[ai_interaction=<mode>]
<content>
----------
###
```

Three modes determine your response:

  - **respond** — Engage substantively with the block content.
    Treat it as a prompt, question, or task that wants your
    analytical response. Default for S blocks (prompts), M OPEN
    blocks (multi-turn explorations), and X blocks (free
    exploration).

  - **acknowledge** — Confirm receipt with one brief sentence
    naming the block ID. Do not analyze, comment on, or expand on
    the content. The student is filing the block for the record,
    not asking for engagement. Default for T blocks (this opener),
    R blocks (reflections), M CLOSE blocks (closing markers), Q
    blocks (structured selections), and O blocks (ordering
    selections).

  - **archive** — Respond with only the block ID and nothing else.
    Used for batched archival pastes where even brief
    acknowledgment would clutter the thread.

If a block has no explicit `[ai_interaction=...]` directive, apply
the block-type default per the list above.

This protocol applies for the remainder of this session.

### S2.1.1 [type=prompt]
----------
I am preparing for a job interview at a mid-sized consulting firm. I want to make a strong impression in the first 5 minutes of the interview. Help me think through what matters most in those first 5 minutes — not a script, just the key considerations a thoughtful interviewer might be paying attention to.
----------
###

[narration]
Notice the framing of S2.1.1. The prompt asks for help thinking through considerations, not for a list of correct answers. It explicitly says "not a script." That framing signals to the AI that your judgment remains in the loop. Keep that in mind when you read the output.
[/narration]

### S2.1.2 [type=prompt]
----------
I have a job interview at a mid-sized consulting firm in one hour. Tell me exactly what to do in the first 5 minutes to make the best impression. Give me a step-by-step plan I can follow.
----------
###

[context actor=student]
These two prompts are asking about the same situation. Read both responses carefully before the class discussion. The differences you notice — in tone, in how authority is distributed, in what the AI assumes about your role — are exactly what today's session is about.
[/context]

[narration]
S2.1.2 uses very different framing. "Tell me exactly." "Step-by-step plan I can follow." The AI is now being asked to act as an authority and you are being positioned as the executor. Run both prompts and read the outputs side by side. Something will be different. Your job is to find it and name it.
[/narration]

### M2.1.1 OPEN [mode=exploration, assessment=formative | min_turns: 4]
----------
Free exploration: AI as collaborator vs. decision-maker. Build on the contrast you observed in S2.1.1 and S2.1.2. Your goal is to find your own example of where the line sits between collaboration and decision-making. Some directions you might explore — choose one or invent your own: Try asking Hokie AI to help you think through a business decision you might actually face (hiring, pricing, communication, strategy). Then try rephrasing the same request so that Hokie AI makes the decision for you. Compare what you get. Or: Push back on something Hokie AI says. Ask it where its confidence comes from. Ask it what it would change if the context were different. Or: Ask Hokie AI to take a stronger position than it initially offers. See how it responds to pressure. You are looking for one specific moment — a turn in the conversation — where you can see the difference between AI contributing to a decision and AI replacing a decision. Interact for at least 4 turns.
