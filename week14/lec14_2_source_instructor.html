BUS 1001 — AI Literacy
Week 14 · Lecture 2
Defending Your Design — Peer Review, Reflection, and Responsibility
Duration: 75 minutes · Platform: Hokie AI · Submission: Thread URL submitted in Canvas before leaving class · Lecture Mode: consolidation
Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:

  1. Explain and defend their simulated agent design decisions to peers using structured criteria.
  2. Critically evaluate a peer's agent using scope, stability, grounding, and accountability as lenses.
  3. Identify features of their own design that overlap with, differ from, or are absent from peer designs.
  4. Reflect on design tradeoffs inherent in agentic system governance.
  5. Connect individual protocol decisions to broader principles of responsible AI deployment.
📋 Note for Students

This is the final assessed lecture of the course. You are not demonstrating a working system — you are defending a designed one. The peer review and reflection today ask you to do something harder than running prompts: articulate why you made the choices you made, what you learned by seeing others' choices, and what remains genuinely uncertain even after 14 weeks of iteration. The quality of your reflection is the quality of your thinking.

◉ Instructor Note

This session has no Hokie AI lab segment. The work is discussion-based and reflective. Your role is to facilitate the peer review rounds with consistent timing, keep feedback focused on design criteria rather than personal preferences, and ensure students have adequate quiet time to write the R block. The most common failure mode today is students writing a summary of their protocol rather than a reflection on what they learned through comparison. Push back gently: "You described your design. Now tell me what you discovered about it by seeing others'."

⏰ Instructor Script
  1. 0:00 Open without preamble: "Today is about defending what you built. Not explaining it — defending it. There is a difference." Pause. "Explaining tells me what you did. Defending tells me why you did it and what you would change." Give students 2 minutes of silence to re-read their Protocol v4 before the peer review begins.
  2. 5:00 Organize groups of 3-4 students. Each student gets exactly 5 minutes to present. Set a visible timer. Tell students: "Your presentation covers four things only — agent identity, one key planning rule, one grounding decision, and one ethical or privacy constraint. That is it. Everything else is context you can discuss in the feedback round."
  3. 8:00 Peer review rounds begin. Rotate so each student presents. During presentations, other group members take brief notes on: one strength, one risk or concern, one question they want to ask.
  4. 35:00 Structured feedback exchange — 15 minutes. Each student receives feedback from their group. Instructor circulates and intervenes only if feedback becomes vague ("it was good") or personal. Redirect to design criteria: "What specifically in the scope definition creates that risk?"
  5. 50:00 Whole-class synthesis — 5 minutes. Instructor asks: "What design patterns came up in multiple groups?" Collect 3-4 observations on the board. These become anchors for the R block.
  6. 55:00 Transition to R block. Tell students: "You are not summarizing your protocol. You are mapping it against what you just heard. Think about four things — what you had in common with everyone, what only you had, what others had that you didn't, and what the group discovered together that no one had alone. That last one is the hardest and the most important."
  7. 57:00 R block begins. Students write in the text box. They have 15 minutes. Minimum 150 words, target 200.
  8. 72:00 FAST CLASS EXTENSION: If students finish early, ask them to add one sentence: "The one thing I would do differently if I started Protocol v1 again, knowing what I know now." This deepens the synthesis without opening a new block.
  9. 73:00 SLOW CLASS FALLBACK: If peer review rounds ran long, reduce R block to 120 words minimum and tell students to focus on the third and fourth Venn regions — what they lacked and what emerged collectively. These are the most analytically demanding and should not be cut.
  10. 74:00 Remind students to submit thread URL to Canvas before leaving. This is the final submission of the course — confirm all students have completed it.

Today's session closes the arc that began in Week 1. You started by learning to read AI output critically. You end by defending a system you designed to govern AI behavior. Between those two points, you built something — iteratively, through failure, through peer feedback, through scenarios that revealed what your protocol could not handle. The reflection today is not a summary of that work. It is a map of what you discovered by placing your work next to someone else's.

💬 Discussion

Two questions to anchor the peer review — hold these in mind throughout:

  • What does it mean for a protocol to be "complete"? Is any protocol ever complete?
  • If you handed your protocol to someone who had never read any of the course material, what would they misunderstand first?
