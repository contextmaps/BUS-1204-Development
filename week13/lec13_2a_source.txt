---
**AI Interaction Protocol**

In this session, you will receive blocks of content with the
following structural pattern:

```
### <BlockID>
----------
<content>
----------
###
```

Some blocks include an interaction-mode directive on a separate
line inside the dashed envelope:

```
### <BlockID>
----------
[ai_interaction=<mode>]
<content>
----------
###
```

Three modes determine your response:

  - **respond** — Engage substantively with the block content.
    Treat it as a prompt, question, or task that wants your
    analytical response. Default for S blocks (prompts), M OPEN
    blocks (multi-turn explorations), and X blocks (free
    exploration).

  - **acknowledge** — Confirm receipt with one brief sentence
    naming the block ID. Do not analyze, comment on, or expand on
    the content. The student is filing the block for the record,
    not asking for engagement. Default for T blocks (this opener),
    R blocks (reflections), M CLOSE blocks (closing markers), Q
    blocks (structured selections), and O blocks (ordering
    selections).

  - **archive** — Respond with only the block ID and nothing else.
    Used for batched archival pastes where even brief
    acknowledgment would clutter the thread.

If a block has no explicit `[ai_interaction=...]` directive, apply
the block-type default per the list above.

This protocol applies for the remainder of this session.

### M13.2a.1 OPEN [mode=consolidation, assessment=formative | min_turns: 3]
----------
Phase 1 of the lab. Paste S13.2a.1 and run the initial task through your deployed team agent. Observe specifically:
- Which grounding source is most active in the response? Is the agent citing specific documents? Following its role/scope instructions? Drawing on context you established in the session opener?
- Where is the response specific to your team's grounding versus generic model output your team did not control?
- Is there a moment where the agent seems to go its own way — answering differently than your grounding would predict?
After the initial response, ask a follow-up question to probe the grounding. For example: "Where did that information come from — is that in our uploaded documents?" or "Why did you frame it that way?" Interact for at least 3 turns.

[rubric id: M13.2a.1]

directive: Evaluate the quality of the student's Phase 1 observation of their deployed team agent's grounding behavior. Assess whether the student observed specific agent behaviors and identified which grounding source produced each behavior, or whether the student described agent behavior in general terms without naming the grounding source responsible.
narration: Phase 1 of the team agent grounding lab. Developing means the student ran the task and accepted the output without observing against grounding source categories — general statements only, or no comparison to what the team configured. Satisfactory means the student observed specific agent behaviors but described them in general terms without naming the grounding source responsible — noticed that the agent gave a different answer than expected or that the response seemed off in some way, but did not identify whether this was because of instructions grounding, knowledge-source grounding, or conversational grounding. Strong means the student observed specific behaviors AND named the grounding source that produced or failed to produce each observation — with direct reference to what the team had configured ("our instructions establish the career navigator role so the tone was right, but we haven't uploaded any internship documents so the specific program recommendations came from general model knowledge, not our grounding").
dimensions: grounding_source_identification, observation_specificity, behavioral_diagnosis, team_configuration_reference
[/rubric]

### M13.2a.2 OPEN [mode=consolidation, assessment=formative | min_turns: 3]
----------
Phase 2 of the lab. Change one grounding source deliberately and observe how your team agent's behavior shifts:
- Add a new knowledge source (upload a document, paste in a specific resource, or add a company guide)
- OR change the conversational frame (provide different context about the user's goals, background, or constraints)
- OR modify the instructions grounding (update the agent's scope, add an escalation rule, change the role)
Run the same task from S13.2a.1 (or a closely related follow-up) through the updated grounding. Observe specifically:
- What changed in the agent's response after the grounding shift?
- Which grounding source produced the change you observed?
- What stayed the same — what behavior was unaffected by the change you made?
Compare Phase 2 behavior to Phase 1 behavior. Interact for at least 3 turns.

[rubric id: R13.2a.1]
type: reflection
context_prompt: S13.2a.1
directive: In 100-140 words, identify the priority observation about how grounding shaped your team agent's behavior in this session. The reflection must reference at least one specific AI interaction moment from the M blocks (a specific response, a behavior shift, a moment of drift or accuracy) and use it to anchor the observation. Conclude with what this session reveals about which grounding source matters most for your team's specific capstone goal.
narration: Lab-specific reflection on team agent grounding behavior. Developing means the student reflects on grounding mechanics conceptually — what instructions, knowledge-source, and conversational grounding mean — without grounding the reflection in a specific moment from the AI interaction in the M blocks. The threshold rule applies: a reflection that does not cite a specific AI interaction moment from the lab is capped at Developing regardless of conceptual quality, because the assignment asks what THIS team agent did in THIS session, not what grounding means in general. Satisfactory means a specific AI interaction moment is cited and used to anchor the observation, and the reflection is written in third-person reportorial voice — describing what the agent did, what the session surfaced, what the grounding change produced — without first-person processing language ("I see how...", "this makes me think...") and without a forward-looking conclusion about what the team should change next. Strong means a specific AI interaction moment is cited, the reflection is anchored in session specifics, and the student extends to an independent insight about team design or capstone execution that the M block exchange did not name.
dimensions: ai_moment_citation, grounding_observation_specificity, lab_grounding, forward_synthesis
[/rubric]
