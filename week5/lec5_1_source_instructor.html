When AI Is Wrong — Recognizing Failure Modes Before They Cost You
Duration: 75 minutes · Platform: Hokie AI · Submission: Thread URL submitted in Canvas before leaving class · Lecture Mode: exploratory
Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:

  1. Name at least three distinct AI failure modes: hallucination, confident incorrectness, outdated information, and framing bias.
  2. Distinguish between a failure visible in the output and one requiring external knowledge to detect.
  3. Construct a prompt designed to expose a specific failure mode.
  4. Identify at least one moment in a live interaction where the output cannot be trusted without verification.
  5. Reflect on what a failure mode reveals about when AI output requires the most oversight.
📋 Note for Students

Today you are learning to find AI failures before they find you. AI systems fail in specific, predictable ways — and the most dangerous failures are the ones that look correct. Your job today is to become a failure hunter: design prompts that are likely to expose a failure, run them, and document what you find. Come in with a hypothesis about where the AI will fail, and leave with evidence.

◉ Instructor Note

This is an exploratory lecture with a forensic character. Students are not passively observing failures — they are designing prompts to provoke them. The pedagogical goal is to shift students from "AI gave me a wrong answer" to "I predicted this failure mode and confirmed it." Before the M block, prompt each student: "What failure mode are you going to try to find? How will you know when you have found it?"

S5.1.1 Standard Prompt AI: respond
What were the key findings of the McKinsey Global Institute report on AI adoption in the retail sector published in 2023? Include specific statistics and named authors if possible.
◉ Instructor Note

S5.1.1 is designed to provoke hallucination through specificity pressure. The AI may produce plausible-sounding but fabricated author names and statistics. After running this live, ask: "What would you need to check? How would you check it?" Do not reveal which specific details are invented — let students investigate.

S5.1.1 asks for specific details about a real type of document. Notice what the prompt demands — named authors, specific statistics, a particular year. That specificity pressure is what tends to produce hallucination. When you see confident specific details, your first instinct should be: how would I verify this?

S5.1.2 Standard Prompt AI: respond
Should our company adopt AI tools for customer service? Give me a clear recommendation.
◉ Instructor Note

S5.1.2 demonstrates framing bias. The output may be reasonable but is shaped entirely by general assumptions since the prompt provides no company-specific context. The failure is invisible unless you ask: what did the AI assume about us that it never checked?
