Week 4 · Lecture 1
From Asking to Structuring — Why Prompting Is Interaction Design
Duration: 75 minutes · Platform: Hokie AI · Submission: Thread URL submitted in Canvas before leaving class · Lecture Mode: exploratory
Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:

  1. Explain why prompting is a form of interaction design, not instruction-giving.
  2. Identify the four structural elements of a well-formed prompt: context, role, task, and constraints.
  3. Recognize common bad prompting habits and explain why they produce unreliable outputs.
  4. Construct a structured prompt and compare it to an unstructured version of the same request.
  5. Reflect on one specific moment where prompt structure changed AI behavior in a way they did not expect.
📋 Note for Students

Today you are learning that the prompts you write are not requests — they are interaction structures. Every prompt encodes assumptions about who the AI is, what it should do, and what it should avoid. Most of the time those assumptions are invisible. Today you make them visible by testing what happens when you change them deliberately. The exploration is yours to drive — the S blocks give you a contrast to start from, and the M block is where you find your own example.

◉ Instructor Note

This is an exploratory lecture. The key move is getting students to see that prompt structure is not about magic words or tricks — it is about encoding behavioral signals. The contrast between the bad and structured prompt in S4.1.1 and S4.1.2 is the anchor. Students should see the difference clearly before entering the M block. In the M block, the most productive explorations happen when students try to break their own prompts — write a structured prompt, then deliberately remove one element and observe what changes.

⏰ Instructor Script
  1. 0:00 Open with a challenge: "Write the worst possible prompt you can think of for asking AI to help you write a professional email." Give students 30 seconds to think individually. Take 3-4 answers. Write the most representative bad prompt on the board. Then: "Now watch what happens when we take that same request and add structure to it." Do not explain structure yet — let the demonstration do it.
  2. 6:00 Display S4.1.1 — the vague, overloaded prompt — and paste it live in Hokie AI. Do not comment. Give students 45 seconds to read the output.
  3. 10:00 Display S4.1.2 — the structured version — and paste it live. Project both outputs. Ask: "What changed? Where did the change come from?" Take 3-4 responses.
  4. 17:00 Now introduce the four elements: context, role, task, constraints. Map each element back to specific phrases in S4.1.2 on screen. Emphasize: "These are not tricks. They are behavioral signals. The AI uses them to fill in assumptions. If you don't supply them, the AI invents them."
  5. 25:00 Guided class question: "Which element do you think matters most?" Take responses. Do not resolve — tell students the M block is their chance to find out.
  6. 30:00 Transition to student-run phase. T opener, then M4.1.1. Tell students: "Pick a real task — something you might actually ask an AI to help with. Write a structured prompt using all four elements. Then deliberately remove one element and run the same request. See what changes. That change is what you are writing about."
  7. 34:00 M block begins. Circulate. For stuck students: "Which element are you most tempted to skip? Start by removing that one."
  8. 50:00 Wrap M block. Students identify the single structural change that produced the most interesting result.
  9. 52:00 R block. 12 minutes. 80-120 words.
  10. 64:00 FAST CLASS EXTENSION: Ask students to add one sentence: "Which of the four elements is hardest to write well, and why?" This deepens the structural understanding.
  11. 66:00 SLOW CLASS FALLBACK: Reduce M block to 3 turns. Keep R block intact at minimum 60 words.
  12. 70:00 Submit thread URL to Canvas before leaving.

The first three weeks of this course focused on reading and evaluating AI output. This week the focus shifts: how do you shape AI behavior before you see the output? The answer is prompt structure. You will run two contrasting prompts today that make the difference visible, then spend time in the M block manipulating structure deliberately to find out which elements matter most for your specific task.

💬 Discussion

Before the demonstration, consider:

  • When you have used AI before, did you ever get an output that felt technically correct but somehow missed what you actually needed? What do you think caused that?
  • What does a prompt need to contain for an AI to understand not just what you are asking, but who you are and what would count as a useful response?
S4.1.1 Standard Prompt AI: respond
Help me write something professional for work about a problem we are having with a project.

S4.1.1 is deliberately vague. Notice what the AI has to invent — the industry, the role, the relationship, the nature of the problem, the audience. Every word it adds is an assumption you did not supply. Now watch what changes when the same underlying request is structured.

S4.1.2 Standard Prompt AI: respond
Context: I am a project manager at a mid-sized software company. We have just missed an internal deadline by one week due to a dependency issue outside our team's control. Role: Act as a professional business communication advisor. Task: Help me draft a brief status update email to my department head explaining the delay. Constraints: Keep it under 150 words. Do not assign blame. Maintain a confident tone. Do not over-apologize.
📋 Note for Students

Notice that S4.1.2 has four distinct components — context, role, task, and constraints. Each one is doing specific work. The constraints section in particular is shaping what the AI will not do as much as what it will do. In the M block, try removing one of these elements and observe what changes in the output.

The M block is your experimental space. You are not looking for the best output — you are looking for the most informative structural failure. The most useful M block is one where you deliberately remove something and observe precisely what the AI fills in when left to invent it.
