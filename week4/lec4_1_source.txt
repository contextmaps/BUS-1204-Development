[objectives]
By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
1. Explain why prompting is a form of interaction design, not instruction-giving.
2. Identify the four structural elements of a well-formed prompt: context, role, task, and constraints.
3. Recognize common bad prompting habits and explain why they produce unreliable outputs.
4. Construct a structured prompt and compare it to an unstructured version of the same request.
5. Reflect on one specific moment where prompt structure changed AI behavior in a way they did not expect.
[/objectives]

[context actor=student]
Today you are learning that the prompts you write are not requests — they are interaction structures. Every prompt encodes assumptions about who the AI is, what it should do, and what it should avoid. Most of the time those assumptions are invisible. Today you make them visible by testing what happens when you change them deliberately. The exploration is yours to drive — the S blocks give you a contrast to start from, and the M block is where you find your own example.
[/context]

[context actor=instructor]

In the M block, the most productive explorations happen when students try to break their own prompts — write a structured prompt, then deliberately remove one element and observe what changes.

[instructor_script]
[0:00] Open with a challenge: "Write the worst possible prompt you can think of for asking AI to help you write a professional email." Give students 30 seconds to think individually. Take 3-4 answers. Write the most representative bad prompt on the board. Then: "Now watch what happens when we take that same request and add structure to it." Do not explain structure yet — let the demonstration do it.
[6:00] Display S4.1.1 — the vague, overloaded prompt — and paste it live in Hokie AI. Do not comment. Give students 45 seconds to read the output.
[10:00] Display S4.1.2 — the structured version — and paste it live. Project both outputs. Ask: "What changed? Where did the change come from?" Take 3-4 responses.
[17:00] Now introduce the four elements: context, role, task, constraints. Map each element back to specific phrases in S4.1.2 on screen. Emphasize: "These are not tricks. They are behavioral signals. The AI uses them to fill in assumptions. If you don't supply them, the AI invents them."
[25:00] Guided class question: "Which element do you think matters most?" Take responses. Do not resolve — tell students the M block is their chance to find out.
[30:00] Transition to student-run phase. R0 opener, then M4.1.1. Tell students: "Pick a real task — something you might actually ask an AI to help with. Write a structured prompt using all four elements. Then deliberately remove one element and run the same request. See what changes. That change is what you are writing about."
[34:00] M block begins. Circulate. For stuck students: "Which element are you most tempted to skip? Start by removing that one."
[50:00] Wrap M block. Students identify the single structural change that produced the most interesting result.
[52:00] R block. 12 minutes. 80-120 words.
[64:00] FAST CLASS EXTENSION: Ask students to add one sentence: "Which of the four elements is hardest to write well, and why?" This deepens the structural understanding.
[66:00] SLOW CLASS FALLBACK: Reduce M block to 3 turns. Keep R block intact at minimum 60 words.
[70:00] Submit thread URL to Canvas before leaving.
[/instructor_script]

### S4.1.1 [type=prompt]
----------
Help me write something professional for work about a problem we are having with a project.
----------
###

[narration]
S4.1.1 is deliberately vague. Notice what the AI has to invent — the industry, the role, the relationship, the nature of the problem, the audience. Every word it adds is an assumption you did not supply. Now watch what changes when the same underlying request is structured.
[/narration]

### S4.1.2 [type=prompt]
----------
Context: I am a project manager at a mid-sized software company. We have just missed an internal deadline by one week due to a dependency issue outside our team's control.
Role: Act as a professional business communication advisor.
Task: Help me draft a brief status update email to my department head explaining the delay.
Constraints: Keep it under 150 words. Do not assign blame. Maintain a confident tone. Do not over-apologize.
----------
###

[context actor=student]
Notice that S4.1.2 has four distinct components — context, role, task, and constraints. Each one is doing specific work. The constraints section in particular is shaping what the AI will not do as much as what it will do. In the M block, try removing one of these elements and observe what changes in the output.
[/context]

[narration]
The M block is your experimental space. You are not looking for the best output — you are looking for the most informative structural failure. The most useful M block is one where you deliberately remove something and observe precisely what the AI fills in when left to invent it.
[/narration]

### M4.1.1 OPEN [mode=exploration, assessment=formative | min_turns: 4]
----------
Free exploration: prompt structure as interaction design. Start with a real task — something you might actually ask an AI to help with in school or work. Write a structured version using all four elements: context, role, task, and constraints. Run it. Then deliberately remove or weaken one element — try removing the constraints, or leaving the role vague, or stripping the context — and run the same core request again. Compare what you get. Then push further: ask Hokie AI to tell you what assumptions it made in the unstructured version that it did not need to make in the structured one. You are looking for one specific structural change that produced a behavioral difference you did not fully predict. That moment is what you will write about. Interact for at least 4 turns.
----------
###
