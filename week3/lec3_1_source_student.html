BUS 1204 — AI Literacy for Business
Week 3 · Lecture 1
Text vs. Multimodal Reasoning — What Changes When AI "Sees"?
Duration: 75 minutes · Platform: Hokie AI · Submission: Thread URL submitted in Canvas before leaving class · Lecture Mode: exploratory
Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:

  1. Distinguish between text-only and multimodal AI outputs at a conceptual level.
  2. Explain how visual outputs change interpretation, persuasion, and risk.
  3. Identify assumptions embedded in AI-generated descriptions of visual content.
  4. Construct a prompt that asks AI to describe or reason about a visual scenario and probe the assumptions it makes.
  5. Reflect on one specific moment where the AI's visual framing revealed a hidden assumption.
📋 Note for Students

Today you are exploring what changes when AI moves from working with text alone to reasoning about visual content. You will not be uploading images — instead, you will ask Hokie AI to describe, imagine, or reason about visual scenarios using language, and then probe what assumptions are embedded in what it produces. The insight you are looking for is this: visuals feel more authoritative than text, and AI knows this. Watch for where that authority gets manufactured.

Last week you identified the line between AI as collaborator and AI as decision-maker. This week the lens shifts: what changes when AI moves from language into the territory of visuals — charts, diagrams, infographics? You will run two prompts today designed to make the difference visible, then spend time in the M block finding your own example. Pay close attention to how certainty feels different in a visual description versus a text-only claim — even when the underlying information is identical.

💬 Discussion

Two questions to hold going into the M block:

  • When AI describes what a chart "would show," is that a fact, an estimate, or an invention? Does the way it describes it make the difference clear?
  • Why might a visual description feel more authoritative than a paragraph making the same claim?
S3.1.1 Standard Prompt
A regional retail company has seen mixed sales results over the past two years — some product categories growing, some declining. Describe the overall sales trend in two to three sentences, including what is driving the pattern.

Read the output from S3.1.1 carefully. Note the qualifiers — words like "likely," "appears to," "suggests." These hedges are doing important work. Now watch what happens to those hedges when we frame the same scenario as a visual.

S3.1.2 Standard Prompt
A regional retail company has seen mixed sales results over the past two years — some product categories growing, some declining. Describe what a clear, professional bar chart showing this trend would look like — including what the key data points would be, what the axes would show, and what a viewer would immediately conclude from it.
📋 Note for Students

Compare the two outputs side by side. Notice where the hedges went in the second response. Notice whether the data points in S3.1.2 are presented as real, estimated, or invented. Notice whether a viewer who saw only the visual description — and not the prompt — would know which it is.

The scenario in both prompts is identical. Only the output format changed — from prose to visual description. Your M block exploration starts from what you noticed in that contrast.

Opening context
Free exploration: text versus visual framing. Build on the contrast you observed in S3.1.1 and S3.1.2. Choose a business scenario relevant to you — a performance metric, a market trend, a comparison between options — and explore what changes when you ask Hokie AI to describe it visually rather than analytically. Some directions to try: Ask Hokie AI to describe a chart or infographic of your scenario, then ask it directly whether the numbers it used are real or invented. Or ask it to describe the same data two ways — once with uncertainty language, once without — and see whether it can do it. Or ask it what a skeptical viewer of the visual description would need to know to evaluate whether the chart is trustworthy. You are looking for one specific moment where visual framing changed the feel of the information — where something felt more certain, more authoritative, or more actionable than the underlying evidence actually supports. Interact for at least 4 turns.
