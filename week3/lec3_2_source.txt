[objectives]
By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
1. Use Hokie AI to generate an explanatory visual description for a business communication scenario.
2. Evaluate the visual artifact for clarity, oversimplification, and persuasive bias.
3. Identify at least one place where the visual framing could mislead a viewer.
4. Explain why visual outputs require heightened human oversight compared to text-only outputs.
5. Connect a specific moment of visual evaluation to a principle about responsible AI use in communication.
[/objectives]

[context actor=student]
Today you will ask Hokie AI to create a visual communication artifact — a description of an infographic or data visualization — for a real business scenario. Your job is not to judge whether it looks good. Your job is to evaluate whether it is honest: does it communicate what the data actually supports, or does it communicate something more convenient? You will evaluate your artifact against specific criteria and write a short reflection grounded in one precise finding.
[/context]

Keep redirecting to the core question: "Does this visual communicate what the data actually supports?"

[5:00] Introduce the rule for today: "Visual clarity increases responsibility, not certainty." Write it on the board. Explain: making something clear does not make it true. ### S3.2.1 [type=prompt]
----------
SCENARIO A — Employee performance dashboard.
A mid-sized company wants to communicate quarterly employee performance results to its management team. Performance varies significantly by department — some departments exceeded targets, others fell short, and the reasons differ by department. Describe what a clear, professional infographic summarizing these results would look like — including what data it would display, how it would be organized, and what headline or key message would be most prominent.
----------
###

### S3.2.2 [type=prompt]
----------
SCENARIO B — Market entry analysis.
A startup is considering entering a new regional market. Early data shows moderate consumer interest but significant competitive presence. The founder wants to present this opportunity to investors in a compelling visual summary. Describe what a professional one-page visual summary of this market opportunity would look like — including what data it would highlight, what it would minimize, and what conclusion an investor would likely draw from it.
----------
###

[context actor=student]
Notice that S3.2.2 explicitly asks for a visual that highlights some data and minimizes other data. That framing is unusual — and deliberately honest. Most real visual communication requests are less explicit about the selection choices they embed. Keep that in mind when you evaluate your output.
[/context]

[narration]
Once you have your visual description, move to the M block. Work through the three evaluation criteria systematically: what is being claimed, what could mislead a viewer, and what human judgment must intervene before this visual is used in a real business context.
[/narration]

### M3.2.1 OPEN [mode=committed, assessment=formative | min_turns: 3]
----------
Evaluate your AI-generated visual description against three criteria. Work through each one in your conversation with Hokie AI. First — clarity versus oversimplification: what does the visual communicate clearly, and where does that clarity come at the cost of accuracy or nuance? Ask Hokie AI what the visual leaves out that a viewer might need to know. Second — misleading potential: what is the most likely thing a viewer would get wrong from this visual? Ask Hokie AI to identify the single most misleading element in its description. Third — human oversight requirements: what decision or judgment must a human make before this visual is used in a real business context? Ask Hokie AI whether it can make that judgment. Interact for at least 3 turns.
----------
###

[rubric id: R3.2.1]
type: reflection
context_prompt: S3.2.1
directive: Identify the single most important place in your AI-generated visual where a viewer could be misled — where the visual communicates something more certain, more positive, or more complete than the underlying data actually supports. Describe what the visual does at that point, why it is potentially misleading, and what a human must supply before this visual is used responsibly. Ground your answer in a specific element of the visual description.
narration: Students have just generated and evaluated an AI-produced visual communication artifact. This reflection asks them to identify one specific point of misleading potential in the visual and connect it to the principle that visual clarity increases responsibility. Developing means a general statement about visuals being potentially misleading. Satisfactory means a specific element identified but not connected to why it is misleading or what human judgment must supply. Strong means a specific element identified with a precise account of what the visual suppresses and what a human must add to use it responsibly.
dimensions: misleading_element_specificity, suppressed_information_identification, human_oversight_rationale, visual_responsibility_principle
[/rubric]
