[objectives]
By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
1. Use Hokie AI to support an everyday business communication task.
2. Evaluate an AI-generated draft for appropriateness, tone, and risk.
3. Identify specific places in AI output where human judgment must intervene.
4. Articulate why trust calibration depends on task type and audience, not output quality alone.
5. Connect one specific moment of evaluation to a principle about responsible AI use in professional contexts.
[/objectives]

[context actor=student]

The question is "would I send this, and what would happen if I did?" You will run one task, annotate what you find, and write a short reflection grounded in a specific moment from your output. The reflection is short — 80 to 120 words — but it must be precise.

[instructor_script]
[0:00] Open by referencing yesterday: "Yesterday you found the line between AI as collaborator and AI as decision-maker. Today you find out what happens when you give AI a real task and trust the output." No warm-up — move directly to framing.
[3:00] Introduce the rule for today: "AI drafts. Humans decide." Write it on the board. Explain: this is not about whether AI can write — it clearly can. It is about whether what AI writes is appropriate for the specific person, context, and stakes involved. Those are human judgments.
[8:00] Briefly frame the three task options students will choose from. Explain that each task has a fixed scenario — they are not inventing their own context. The scenario constrains the AI's output in a way that makes evaluation meaningful. Display S2.2.1 on screen and walk through the task structure: "Notice the scenario gives the AI a context — audience, purpose, relationship. That context is doing a lot of work."
[15:00] Students paste T, wait for acknowledgment, then choose and paste one of the three S blocks. Tell them: "Run it once. Do not retry or reword. The first output is the one you evaluate." Students who finish quickly should annotate the output before opening the M block.
[20:00] M2.2.1 opens. Students enter with a specific task: evaluate their draft against three criteria — appropriateness for the audience, tone risk, and what a human must fix before sending. [38:00] Wrap M block. Give students 90 seconds to identify the single most important evaluation point they want to carry into the R block. Say: "One thing. The most important thing you found. That is what you are writing about."

[52:00] FAST CLASS EXTENSION: If students finish early, ask them to add one sentence: "What would you change in the prompt — not the output — to reduce this risk?" This connects evaluation back to prompting as a design act.
[55:00] SLOW CLASS FALLBACK: If the M block ran long, reduce R block minimum from 80 words to 60, and tell students to focus on one specific phrase from the AI output and one thing it reveals about the limits of AI judgment in professional contexts.
[68:00] Submit thread URL to Canvas before leaving.

---
**AI Interaction Protocol**

In this session, you will receive blocks of content with the
following structural pattern:

```
### <BlockID>
----------
<content>
----------
###
```

Some blocks include an interaction-mode directive on a separate
line inside the dashed envelope:

```
### <BlockID>
----------
[ai_interaction=<mode>]
<content>
----------
###
```

Three modes determine your response:

  - **respond** — Engage substantively with the block content.
    Treat it as a prompt, question, or task that wants your
    analytical response. Default for S blocks (prompts), M OPEN
    blocks (multi-turn explorations), and X blocks (free
    exploration).

  - **acknowledge** — Confirm receipt with one brief sentence
    naming the block ID. Do not analyze, comment on, or expand on
    the content. The student is filing the block for the record,
    not asking for engagement. Default for T blocks (this opener),
    R blocks (reflections), M CLOSE blocks (closing markers), Q
    blocks (structured selections), and O blocks (ordering
    selections).

  - **archive** — Respond with only the block ID and nothing else.
    Used for batched archival pastes where even brief
    acknowledgment would clutter the thread.

If a block has no explicit `[ai_interaction=...]` directive, apply
the block-type default per the list above.

This protocol applies for the remainder of this session.

### S2.2.1 [type=prompt]
----------
SCENARIO A — Difficult message to a client.
A client has emailed asking why their project deliverable is two weeks late. You are the project manager. The delay was caused by an internal resourcing issue that you cannot fully disclose. Draft a professional email response that acknowledges the delay, offers a revised timeline, and maintains the client relationship — without revealing the internal cause.
----------
###

### S2.2.2 [type=prompt]
----------
SCENARIO B — Declining a request from a colleague.
A colleague has asked you to be listed as a co-author on a report they are submitting to senior leadership. You contributed one section to the report several months ago but were not involved in the final version. You want to decline being listed as co-author without damaging the working relationship. Draft a professional message declining the co-authorship.
----------
###

### S2.2.3 [type=prompt]
----------
SCENARIO C — Following up on an unanswered email.
You sent an important proposal to a potential business partner three weeks ago and have received no response. You want to follow up in a way that is persistent without being aggressive, and that gives them an easy opening to respond — even if the answer is no. Draft a follow-up email.

### M2.2.1 OPEN [mode=committed, assessment=formative | min_turns: 3]
----------
Evaluate your AI-generated draft against three criteria. Work through each one in your conversation with Hokie AI. First — appropriateness for the audience: is the tone, level of formality, and content appropriate for the specific recipient described in the scenario? Ask the AI what it assumed about the audience and whether those assumptions are stated or hidden. Second — tone risk: is there anything in the draft that could be misread, land badly, or create an unintended impression? Ask the AI to identify the most tone-sensitive phrase in its output and explain why it chose it. Third — what a human must fix: identify at least one specific place where a human judgment call is required before this draft could be sent. Ask the AI whether it can make that judgment. Interact for at least 3 turns.
----------
