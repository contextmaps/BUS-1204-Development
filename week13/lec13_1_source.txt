[objectives]
By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
1. Define grounding in the context of AI agents and explain why every agent has grounding whether or not the designer chose it deliberately.
2. Distinguish three grounding sources — instructions grounding, knowledge-source grounding, and conversational grounding — and describe how each shapes agent behavior differently.
3. Identify, for their specific team capstone domain, what each grounding source currently contains and what it does not yet contain.
4. Articulate which grounding source is likely to be most consequential for their team's capstone goal and why.
5. Write a reflection that reports on the M block diagnostic and identifies the grounding source they expect to matter most.
[/objectives]

Your agent's behavior is shaped not only by its instructions but by the specific files, documents, and conversational context you give it. Today you learn to see these as three distinct grounding sources and to assess what your team's agent currently has in each category.

[instructor_script]
[0:00] Open with a question: "When your team agent answers a question, where does the answer come from?" Take a few responses. Write three columns on the board: INSTRUCTIONS | FILES & DOCUMENTS | THIS CONVERSATION. Tell the class these are the three grounding sources. "Everything your agent knows comes from one of these three places. If you didn't put something there, and it wasn't in the conversation, the agent is drawing on whatever the underlying model already knows — which is not something you controlled."
[8:00] Introduce the three grounding sources:
  INSTRUCTIONS GROUNDING: The system prompt, protocol, role, and rules you wrote. This shapes default behavior across every task. It's the "who is this agent" layer.
  KNOWLEDGE-SOURCE GROUNDING: Uploaded files, linked documents, retrieval-augmented content. This gives the agent specific reference material it can cite. It's the "what does this agent know" layer.
  CONVERSATIONAL GROUNDING: What the user establishes in the first few turns of a session — their name, their major, their goal, the specific task context. This persists within a session but resets when the session ends. It's the "what has this agent learned today" layer.
[18:00] Worked contrast. "Here is the same task run through three versions of the same agent: bare LLM with no grounding, agent with instructions only, agent with instructions plus knowledge source." Walk through the contrast on screen. Show how the bare LLM gives a generic answer. The instructions-only agent responds in the right role and tone but still draws on generic knowledge for facts. The instructions-plus-knowledge agent cites the specific document. "The grounding source change changed the quality of the response — not the instructions, the knowledge."
[30:00] Key point: "Grounding is not optional. If you haven't chosen your knowledge-source grounding, the platform chose it for you. Default is a grounding decision too."
[35:00] Pair discussion. "With your team partner nearest you: Take your team agent. For each of the three grounding sources — instructions, knowledge, conversational — what is currently in it? What is missing? If a student using your agent asks something outside your instructions, where does the answer come from?"

[62:00] Wrap M13.1.1. Brief pull: "Which grounding source do you think is currently the weakest for your team's agent? Instructions, knowledge sources, or conversational?" Take a few responses.
[65:00] R13.1.1 reflection. 10 minutes. 80-120 words. Identify which grounding source you expect to be most consequential for your team's capstone goal.
[72:00] Transition cue: "In lec13_2 you will deploy grounding in the lab. Today is the map — you now know the three sources and where your team's agent currently stands on each."

### S13.1.1 [type=prompt]
----------
Your team has built a shared capstone agent. Today you take stock of its grounding — where its knowledge actually comes from — across three specific sources.

INSTRUCTIONS GROUNDING: What is in your agent's system prompt and protocol? This is the "who is this agent" layer. Instructions grounding shapes the agent's default behavior across all tasks: its role, tone, scope rules, and escalation logic. It does NOT give the agent specific facts — it tells the agent how to behave.

KNOWLEDGE-SOURCE GROUNDING: What documents, files, or retrieval content has your team uploaded or linked? This is the "what does this agent know" layer. If your team has not uploaded anything, this layer is empty — or filled by whatever the platform provides by default, which is not something you controlled.

CONVERSATIONAL GROUNDING: What context does the user establish in the first few turns of a session? This is the "what has this agent learned today" layer. Conversational grounding is powerful but temporary — it resets when the session ends.

Describe your team's capstone agent to the AI. Then work through a three-source inventory:
1. For instructions grounding: What specifically is in it? What is it missing?
2. For knowledge-source grounding: What specifically has your team uploaded or linked? If nothing — what does the agent rely on instead?
3. For conversational grounding: What context do you expect users to provide? Is that designed into the session opener, or left to chance?

After the inventory, identify: which grounding source do you expect to be the most consequential for your team's specific capstone goal, and why?

### M13.1.1 OPEN [mode=exploration, assessment=formative | min_turns: 4]
----------
Work through the three-source grounding inventory of your team's capstone agent. Begin by describing the agent — its purpose, its domain, and who uses it. Then iterate through the inventory:

1. Instructions grounding: What is in it? What is missing? Ask the AI: "What would my agent do if a user asked something specific that my instructions don't cover?"
2. Knowledge-source grounding: What has your team actually uploaded or linked? If the answer is "nothing yet," ask the AI: "What would happen if a user asked for a specific fact — where does the answer come from when there are no documents?"
3. Conversational grounding: What user-provided context does your agent rely on? Ask the AI: "What's the minimum context a user needs to provide at the start of a session for my agent to be useful?"

By the end, identify which grounding source you expect to be most consequential for your team's specific capstone goal. Interact for at least 4 turns.
----------

### M13.1.1 CLOSE
###

[rubric id: M13.1.1]
type: m_block
context_prompt: S13.1.1
directive: Evaluate the quality of the student's three-source grounding inventory for their team capstone agent. Assess whether the student engaged specifically with all three grounding sources (instructions, knowledge-source, conversational) using their actual team agent as the object of analysis, identified what is currently present and currently missing in each source, and arrived at a specific conclusion about which source is most consequential for their capstone goal with a reasoned explanation. The Strong inventory both maps what currently exists and names the specific gap most likely to affect the team's capstone outcomes.
narration: Students take a three-source inventory of their team capstone agent's grounding. Developing means the student discussed grounding sources abstractly without mapping them to their actual team agent, or declared their instructions thorough without probing what happens when users ask for specific facts outside the protocol. Satisfactory means the student engaged their team agent specifically and identified limits in at least one grounding source, but did not complete the inventory across all three sources or did not arrive at a reasoned conclusion about which source is most consequential. Strong means the student engaged their team agent against all three grounding sources, identified specific gaps with concrete evidence (e.g., "our instructions establish the role but we have no uploaded documents — if a user asks about a specific company program, the agent draws on model priors we did not choose"), and named the most consequential grounding source with a reason tied to their team's specific capstone goal.
dimensions: source_coverage, grounding_gap_identification, capstone_domain_specificity, consequence_reasoning
[/rubric]

type: reflection

directive: In 80-120 words, reflect on which grounding source you expect to be most consequential for your team's capstone goal and why. The reflection should identify a specific grounding source (instructions, knowledge-source, or conversational), explain why that source matters most for your team's domain, and connect the reasoning to the specifics of your team's design. The reflection should go beyond restating the inventory — it should make a reasoned argument for prioritization.
narration: Reflection on grounding source prioritization. Developing means the student reflects on the three grounding sources at the concept level — explaining what instructions, knowledge-source, and conversational grounding mean — without grounding the reflection in a specific moment from the M13.1.1 exchange. The threshold rule applies: a reflection that does not cite a specific AI interaction moment from the M block is capped at Developing regardless of conceptual quality or prioritization reasoning, because the assignment asks what THIS diagnostic revealed, not what grounding sources mean in general. Satisfactory means the student references a specific AI interaction moment from M13.1.1 and uses it to anchor the prioritization argument, without extending to a forward-looking conclusion. Strong means a specific AI interaction moment is cited, used to anchor the argument, and the student extends to an independent insight — an implication for team design or capstone execution that the AI did not name.
dimensions: ai_moment_specificity, grounding_source_prioritization, capstone_domain_connection, independent_extension
