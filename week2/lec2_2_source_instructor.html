BUS 1204 — AI Literacy for Business
Week 2 · Lecture 2
Practical Business Tasks — Calibrating Trust in AI Outputs
Duration: 75 minutes · Platform: Hokie AI · Submission: Thread URL submitted in Canvas before leaving class · Lecture Mode: consolidation
Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:

  1. Use Hokie AI to support an everyday business communication task.
  2. Evaluate an AI-generated draft for appropriateness, tone, and risk.
  3. Identify specific places in AI output where human judgment must intervene.
  4. Articulate why trust calibration depends on task type and audience, not output quality alone.
  5. Connect one specific moment of evaluation to a principle about responsible AI use in professional contexts.
📋 Note for Students

Today you will run a real business communication task in Hokie AI and evaluate what comes back — not for polish, but for risk. The question is not "is this well written?" The question is "would I send this, and what would happen if I did?" You will run one task, annotate what you find, and write a short reflection grounded in a specific moment from your output. The reflection is short — 80 to 120 words — but it must be precise.

◉ Instructor Note

This is a consolidation lecture. Students ran exploratory AI interactions yesterday and identified the collaborator-versus-decision-maker distinction. Today they apply that thinking to a concrete artifact — an AI-generated business communication draft — and evaluate it as a professional would. The most important concept to reinforce during the lab segment is that "sounds good" and "is appropriate to send" are different judgments. Circulate during the M block and ask students: "Would you put your name on this? Why or why not?" That question tends to surface the most productive analysis.

⏰ Instructor Script
  1. 0:00 Open by referencing yesterday: "Yesterday you found the line between AI as collaborator and AI as decision-maker. Today you find out what happens when you give AI a real task and trust the output." No warm-up — move directly to framing.
  2. 3:00 Introduce the rule for today: "AI drafts. Humans decide." Write it on the board. Explain: this is not about whether AI can write — it clearly can. It is about whether what AI writes is appropriate for the specific person, context, and stakes involved. Those are human judgments.
  3. 8:00 Briefly frame the three task options students will choose from. Explain that each task has a fixed scenario — they are not inventing their own context. The scenario constrains the AI's output in a way that makes evaluation meaningful. Display S2.2.1 on screen and walk through the task structure: "Notice the scenario gives the AI a context — audience, purpose, relationship. That context is doing a lot of work."
  4. 15:00 Students paste R0, wait for acknowledgment, then choose and paste one of the three S blocks. Tell them: "Run it once. Do not retry or reword. The first output is the one you evaluate." Students who finish quickly should annotate the output before opening the M block.
  5. 20:00 M2.2.1 opens. Students enter with a specific task: evaluate their draft against three criteria — appropriateness for the audience, tone risk, and what a human must fix before sending. Circulate and ask the question: "Would you put your name on this?" Students should be actively identifying specific phrases or choices in the output, not general impressions.
  6. 38:00 Wrap M block. Give students 90 seconds to identify the single most important evaluation point they want to carry into the R block. Say: "One thing. The most important thing you found. That is what you are writing about."
  7. 40:00 R block begins. Students write in the text box. 12 minutes. Target 80-120 words.
  8. 52:00 FAST CLASS EXTENSION: If students finish early, ask them to add one sentence: "What would you change in the prompt — not the output — to reduce this risk?" This connects evaluation back to prompting as a design act.
  9. 55:00 SLOW CLASS FALLBACK: If the M block ran long, reduce R block minimum from 80 words to 60, and tell students to focus on one specific phrase from the AI output and one thing it reveals about the limits of AI judgment in professional contexts.
  10. 68:00 Submit thread URL to Canvas before leaving.

Opening context
Evaluate your AI-generated draft against three criteria. Work through each one in your conversation with Hokie AI. First — appropriateness for the audience: is the tone, level of formality, and content appropriate for the specific recipient described in the scenario? Ask the AI what it assumed about the audience and whether those assumptions are stated or hidden. Second — tone risk: is there anything in the draft that could be misread, land badly, or create an unintended impression? Ask the AI to identify the most tone-sensitive phrase in its output and explain why it chose it. Third — what a human must fix: identify at least one specific place where a human judgment call is required before this draft could be sent. Ask the AI whether it can make that judgment. Interact for at least 3 turns.
