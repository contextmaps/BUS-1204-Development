Grounding Mechanics — Where Does Your Agent's Knowledge Come From?
Duration: 75 minutes · Platform: Hokie AI (default thread — reasoning partner) · Submission: Thread URL submitted in Canvas before leaving class · Lecture Mode: exploratory
Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:

  1. Define grounding in the context of AI agents and explain why every agent has grounding whether or not the designer chose it deliberately.
  2. Distinguish three grounding sources — instructions grounding, knowledge-source grounding, and conversational grounding — and describe how each shapes agent behavior differently.
  3. Identify, for their specific team capstone domain, what each grounding source currently contains and what it does not yet contain.
  4. Articulate which grounding source is likely to be most consequential for their team's capstone goal and why.
  5. Write a reflection that reports on the M block diagnostic and identifies the grounding source they expect to matter most.
📋 Note for Students

Your team has been building a shared agent. Until now, the main design decisions have been about the agent's instructions — its role, protocol, and rules. Today the course introduces a different question: what does the agent actually know, and where does that knowledge come from? Grounding is the answer. Your agent's behavior is shaped not only by its instructions but by the specific files, documents, and conversational context you give it. Today you learn to see these as three distinct grounding sources and to assess what your team's agent currently has in each category.

◉ Instructor Note

This is the conceptual vocabulary lecture for grounding mechanics, preparing students for the team agent lab in lec13_2 and the grounding deployment in lec13_2a. The pedagogical move is to make the invisible visible: students think they designed their agent's behavior through protocol language, but much of what the agent actually does is shaped by what it knows — and right now most teams have only addressed instructions grounding. The productive friction comes when students realize that their team agent's knowledge-source grounding is either empty (they haven't uploaded anything) or default (the platform provides it). Help students in the M block see the difference between "I told the agent what to do" and "I gave the agent what it needs to know." The target by the end of M13.1.1 is a concrete three-source inventory of the team agent's grounding — specific about what exists, specific about what is missing.

⏰ Instructor Script
  1. 0:00 Open with a question: "When your team agent answers a question, where does the answer come from?" Take a few responses. Write three columns on the board: INSTRUCTIONS | FILES & DOCUMENTS | THIS CONVERSATION. Tell the class these are the three grounding sources. "Everything your agent knows comes from one of these three places. If you didn't put something there, and it wasn't in the conversation, the agent is drawing on whatever the underlying model already knows — which is not something you controlled."
  2. 8:00 Introduce the three grounding sources:
  3. INSTRUCTIONS GROUNDING: The system prompt, protocol, role, and rules you wrote. This shapes default behavior across every task. It's the "who is this agent" layer.
  4. KNOWLEDGE-SOURCE GROUNDING: Uploaded files, linked documents, retrieval-augmented content. This gives the agent specific reference material it can cite. It's the "what does this agent know" layer.
  5. CONVERSATIONAL GROUNDING: What the user establishes in the first few turns of a session — their name, their major, their goal, the specific task context. This persists within a session but resets when the session ends. It's the "what has this agent learned today" layer.
  6. 18:00 Worked contrast. "Here is the same task run through three versions of the same agent: bare LLM with no grounding, agent with instructions only, agent with instructions plus knowledge source." Walk through the contrast on screen. Show how the bare LLM gives a generic answer. The instructions-only agent responds in the right role and tone but still draws on generic knowledge for facts. The instructions-plus-knowledge agent cites the specific document. "The grounding source change changed the quality of the response — not the instructions, the knowledge."
  7. 30:00 Key point: "Grounding is not optional. If you haven't chosen your knowledge-source grounding, the platform chose it for you. Default is a grounding decision too."
  8. 35:00 Pair discussion. "With your team partner nearest you: Take your team agent. For each of the three grounding sources — instructions, knowledge, conversational — what is currently in it? What is missing? If a student using your agent asks something outside your instructions, where does the answer come from?"
  9. 40:00 Students paste R0. Default HokieAI thread.
  10. 42:00 Students paste S13.1.1. Walk through on screen.
  11. 45:00 M13.1.1 begins. Students work through the three-source inventory with the AI. Min 4 turns. Circulate. Listen for students who say "our instructions cover everything" — push them to ask what happens when a user asks for a specific fact that is not in the protocol.
  12. 62:00 Wrap M13.1.1. Brief pull: "Which grounding source do you think is currently the weakest for your team's agent? Instructions, knowledge sources, or conversational?" Take a few responses.
  13. 65:00 R13.1.1 reflection. 10 minutes. 80-120 words. Identify which grounding source you expect to be most consequential for your team's capstone goal.
  14. 72:00 Transition cue: "In lec13_2 you will deploy grounding in the lab. Today is the map — you now know the three sources and where your team's agent currently stands on each."
  15. 75:00 Submit thread URL to Canvas.

Grounding is not a technical feature — it is a design decision. Every agent has grounding: the instructions you wrote, the documents you uploaded, and the context the user provides. If you did not choose your grounding deliberately, you inherited the platform default. Today you take stock of where your team agent's grounding currently stands across all three sources and identify which one will matter most for your capstone goal.
