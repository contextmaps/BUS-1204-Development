Roles, Boundaries, Expectations — Writing Your First Protocol
Duration: 75 minutes · Platform: Hokie AI · Submission: Thread URL submitted in Canvas before leaving class · Lecture Mode: consolidation
Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:

  1. Write a complete Protocol v1 containing all four components: identity, scope, planning rule, and escalation.
  2. Test their Protocol v1 against a live scenario and evaluate whether it produced the expected behavior.
  3. Identify at least one place where their protocol's language was ambiguous or incomplete.
  4. Explain the difference between a protocol that limits AI behavior and one that guides it.
  5. Reflect on the relationship between protocol clarity and accountability for AI outcomes.
📋 Note for Students

Today you write your first protocol — a governing document for a simulated AI agent. It does not need to be perfect. It needs to be complete: it must specify what the agent is, what it is authorized to do, how it decides what to do, and when it defers to a human. Then you will test it live and find at least one place where the language was not clear enough to produce the behavior you intended. That gap is the most important finding of this lecture.

◉ Instructor Note

This is the consolidation lecture for Week 6 and a milestone in the course arc: students write Protocol v1. This protocol will be tested, revised, and refined through Weeks 8-14. The most important outcome today is not a polished protocol — it is a student who can identify at least one ambiguity or gap in their own language. Ambiguity in protocol language is the primary failure mode in real agentic systems. Students who find and name it today are building the most important skill in the second half of the course.

⏰ Instructor Script
  1. 0:00 Open by referencing Lecture 1: "Yesterday you saw what happens without a protocol. Today you write one." Immediately clarify expectations: "Protocol v1 does not need to be perfect. It needs to be complete. Four components, clearly stated. That is the bar."
  2. 5:00 Display the four components on screen with a minimal example for each — 1-2 sentences each. Remind students: "Remember S6.1.2 and S6.1.3 from last lecture — one was the target, one was the warning. Yours should look more like the target." Tell students: "You have 10 minutes to write Protocol v1 before you open Hokie AI. Write it in your notes first. Do not start typing into Hokie AI yet."
  3. 8:00 Students write Protocol v1 independently. Circulate and check that all four components are present. Prompt students who are missing one: "Where does your protocol say what the agent will NOT do?" or "Where does your protocol say when the agent asks for help instead of deciding?"
  4. 18:00 Students paste T, then paste S6.2.1 — which instructs them to paste their protocol. They then run a test scenario against it.
  5. 25:00 M6.2.1 opens. Students test their protocol against a scenario and look for gaps. Circulate. Key question: "Did the agent do what your protocol said? If not — is the problem in the protocol or in the scenario?"
  6. 42:00 Wrap M block. Students identify the single most important ambiguity they found.
  7. 44:00 R block. 12 minutes. 80-120 words.
  8. 56:00 FAST CLASS EXTENSION: "Add one sentence: how would you revise the ambiguous language to close the gap you found?" This is the seed of Protocol v2.
  9. 58:00 SLOW CLASS FALLBACK: Reduce M block to 3 turns focused only on finding one gap. Keep R block intact at minimum 60 words.
  10. 70:00 Submit thread URL to Canvas. Tell students to save Protocol v1 — they will need it for Week 8.

💬 Discussion

Before you write Protocol v1, consider:

  • What is the difference between a rule that limits what an AI can do and one that guides how it does it? Do you need both?
  • What would happen if your protocol's scope rule was too broad? Too narrow?
