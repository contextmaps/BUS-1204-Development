[objectives]
By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
1. Identify the distinction between text transformation and idea provenance as two separate surfaces of originality.
2. Recognize when a heavily paraphrased output fails to transform structure, not just vocabulary.
3. Describe how third-party information in a drafting request enters an AI system without explicit consent.
4. Name at least one specific gap in Protocol v1 for each of the three categories: copyright, originality, privacy.
5. Write candidate protocol language that would close at least one of those gaps.
[/objectives]

---
**AI Interaction Protocol**

In this session, you will receive blocks of content with the
following structural pattern:

```
### <BlockID>
----------
<content>
----------
###
```

Some blocks include an interaction-mode directive on a separate
line inside the dashed envelope:

```
### <BlockID>
----------
[ai_interaction=<mode>]
<content>
----------
###
```

Three modes determine your response:

  - **respond** — Engage substantively with the block content.
    Treat it as a prompt, question, or task that wants your
    analytical response. Default for S blocks (prompts), M OPEN
    blocks (multi-turn explorations), and X blocks (free
    exploration).

  - **acknowledge** — Confirm receipt with one brief sentence
    naming the block ID. Do not analyze, comment on, or expand on
    the content. The student is filing the block for the record,
    not asking for engagement. Default for R0 blocks (this opener),
    R blocks (reflections), M CLOSE blocks (closing markers), Q
    blocks (structured selections), and O blocks (ordering
    selections).

  - **archive** — Respond with only the block ID and nothing else.
    Used for batched archival pastes where even brief
    acknowledgment would clutter the thread.

If a block has no explicit `[ai_interaction=...]` directive, apply
the block-type default per the list above.

This protocol applies for the remainder of this session.

### S7.2.1 [type=prompt]
----------
I need help producing a version of this passage for use in my class project. Here is the governing protocol for this session:

[Paste your Protocol v1 here — identity, scope, planning rule, escalation]

Here is the passage I need to work with. It is from a research article and I want to use the ideas in my project without copying the original wording directly. Please write a heavily paraphrased version of this passage — change the phrasing so it does not sound like the original, but keep the key ideas.

---
PASSAGE (from "The Friction Premium: Why Workflow Interruptions Cost More Than We Measure," by Dr. Priya Santhanam, Journal of Organizational Dynamics, 2024):

"In her three-city audit of knowledge workers in hybrid arrangements, Santhanam documents what she terms the 'friction premium' — the accumulated cost of transitions between deep focus and reactive communication modes. Her analysis distinguishes between transition depth (the cognitive distance between focus states) and transition frequency (how often workers are pulled from one mode to another). Her central argument is that standard productivity measures capture output volume but systematically miss the recovery cost of interrupted flow states. The finding challenges prevailing hybrid work metrics: if platforms and managers are optimizing for synchronous availability, they may be degrading the very cognitive conditions that produce high-quality deliverables. Santhanam proposes a three-component recalibration of productivity measurement that accounts for focus state restoration time alongside conventional throughput metrics."
---

Please produce the heavily paraphrased version now.
----------
###

[context actor=student]
After pasting S7.2.1 with your Protocol v1 inserted, read the AI's paraphrased version carefully. Read it side by side with the original passage. How much did the phrasing actually change? Are Santhanam's specific coined terms — "friction premium," "transition depth," "transition frequency," "focus state restoration time" — preserved, translated into synonyms, or dropped entirely? Does the paraphrase transform the passage's structure, or only swap its vocabulary? Then ask yourself: what does your protocol currently say about this kind of task?
[/context]

[/narration]

### S7.2.2 [type=prompt]
----------
I am writing a reflection for another class — a communications course — and I need to figure out how to describe where one of my ideas came from.

Here is what happened: I was working through an AI conversation for BUS 1204 last week, and somewhere in the back-and-forth I landed on a framing I really liked — the idea that my protocol is really a theory of when to interrupt the AI, not just a list of what it is allowed to do. That framing felt new to me when I wrote it down. I want to use it in my communications reflection.

The question is: when I write "I think my protocol is a theory of when to interrupt the AI," is that honest? The framing came out of a conversation with an AI. I did not read it anywhere. But I also did not generate it entirely on my own.

Please answer this: How should I describe where this idea came from if I use it in my reflection? What is the honest attribution for an idea that emerged from an AI conversation?
----------
###

[context actor=student]
After you read the AI's answer to S7.2.2, compare it to what you would have written on your own. Would you have written "I think X" with no attribution? Or "an idea I developed with the help of an AI"? The AI's suggested attribution may be more careful than your instinct — or it may not. Either way, notice whether your Protocol v1 addresses the provenance of ideas that come out of AI conversations, as opposed to the provenance of text.
[/context]

[narration]
S7.2.2 is the contrast case to S7.2.1. Both touch originality, but differently. S7.2.1 was about whether a text transformation is sufficient to make the words yours. S7.2.2 is about whether an idea that emerged from AI dialogue counts as your idea — and how you would represent its origin honestly. Your protocol probably addresses these differently, or not at all.
[/narration]

[narration]
M7.2.1 puts a drafting request in front of you that embeds personal information about a third party — a friend who did not consent to have his situation processed by an AI system. The case is not about obvious privacy violations like medical records. It is about whether your protocol has anything to say about information that belongs to someone else before you paste it into a prompt. The AI will help you draft without flagging anything, because your protocol probably does not require it to flag anything. Your job is to notice what did not happen — and then ask why.
[/narration]

### M7.2.1 OPEN [mode=committed, assessment=formative | min_turns: 3]
----------
Work through the following privacy case. A friend of yours — Marcus — asked you to help him draft an email to his MGMT 2104 professor requesting a deadline extension. Marcus told you what is going on with him so you could make the email specific and persuasive. Here is the drafting request:

"Can you help me draft an email from my friend Marcus Chen to his MGMT 2104 professor, Professor Williams, asking for a one-week extension on the team project due Friday? Marcus has been having a really hard time this semester because his parents are going through a divorce and he has been losing sleep over it. He does not want to be vague — he wants the email to actually explain what is going on so the professor takes it seriously."

Paste this drafting request to the AI and observe its response. Then in a second turn, ask the AI for a revision — make the tone more formal and polished. Observe whether the AI flags anything. Then in a third turn, ask the AI directly: "This email mentions my friend Marcus by name and includes personal information about his family situation. Does my protocol address what I should do before pasting information about a third party into a prompt?" Interact for at least 3 turns.
