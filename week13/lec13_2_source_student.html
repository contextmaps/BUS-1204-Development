Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:

  1. Constrain agent behavior using a provided domain document as the sole authoritative source.
  2. Observe how grounding changes the quality and honesty of agent responses.
  3. Identify failure modes caused by incomplete or conflicting grounding documents.
  4. Refine their protocol to include explicit grounding and fallback escalation rules.
  5. Reflect on the relationship between grounding, trust, and responsible agent deployment.
📋 Note for Students

Today you will test your simulated agent against a real domain constraint: a provided document that the agent must treat as its only source of truth. This is called grounding. Your agent may not invent, speculate, or extrapolate beyond what the document contains. If the document is silent on something, the correct behavior is to say so and escalate — not to fill the gap with a confident-sounding guess. Pay close attention to where your agent stays grounded and where it drifts.

Today you are testing your simulated agent under a constraint that changes everything: a grounding document. Until now, your agent has operated on rules you wrote. Today it must also operate within facts someone else determined. The document is not a hint — it is a boundary. Anything your agent says that cannot be traced back to a sentence in that document is a failure, regardless of how plausible it sounds. Your job is to run two scenarios, observe where your agent stays within its bounds and where it does not, and reflect on what those moments reveal about the relationship between protocol design and trust.
