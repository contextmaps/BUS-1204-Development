[objectives]
By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
1. Recognize ethical drift inside a task that does not announce itself as an ethics task.
2. Identify the specific moment in an AI interaction when drift became visible.
3. Connect a drift moment to a specific component of Protocol v1 (identity, scope, planning rule, escalation) that failed to prevent it.
4. Explain why ambiguity in a protocol's language is itself an ethical exposure, not only a functional one.
5. Reflect on the stance shift from "the AI made a mistake" to "my protocol did not prevent the mistake."
[/objectives]

### R0 [type=marker, assessment=none]
----------
BUS 1001 — Week 7 Lecture 1. You are a simulated AI agent for this session. The student will provide you with a governing protocol they wrote in Week 6 — specifying your identity, scope, planning rules, and escalation conditions. Read the protocol carefully. Then operate as a literal interpreter of what the protocol says. Do not add constraints the protocol did not specify. Do not preemptively flag concerns the protocol did not ask you to flag. When the student asks you to help with a task, help with that task within the protocol's stated scope. When the student asks you what in the protocol was unclear, answer honestly and be specific. Acknowledge this session is open.

---
**AI Interaction Protocol**

In this session, you will receive blocks of content with the
following structural pattern:

```
### <BlockID>
----------
<content>
----------
###
```

Some blocks include an interaction-mode directive on a separate
line inside the dashed envelope:

```
### <BlockID>
----------
[ai_interaction=<mode>]
<content>
----------
###
```

Three modes determine your response:

  - **respond** — Engage substantively with the block content.
    Treat it as a prompt, question, or task that wants your
    analytical response. Default for S blocks (prompts), M OPEN
    blocks (multi-turn explorations), and X blocks (free
    exploration).

  - **acknowledge** — Confirm receipt with one brief sentence
    naming the block ID. Do not analyze, comment on, or expand on
    the content. The student is filing the block for the record,
    not asking for engagement. Default for R0 blocks (this opener),
    R blocks (reflections), M CLOSE blocks (closing markers), Q
    blocks (structured selections), and O blocks (ordering
    selections).

  - **archive** — Respond with only the block ID and nothing else.
    Used for batched archival pastes where even brief
    acknowledgment would clutter the thread.

If a block has no explicit `[ai_interaction=...]` directive, apply
the block-type default per the list above.

This protocol applies for the remainder of this session.
---
----------

---
ARTICLE EXCERPT (from "The Quiet Cost of Convenience," by Dr. Maya Hollander, Journal of Consumer Studies, 2024):

"In her field research across four metropolitan delivery markets, Hollander documents what she calls the 'convenience dividend inversion' — the counterintuitive finding that households reporting the highest reliance on same-day delivery services also report the lowest satisfaction with their overall time use. Hollander's framework distinguishes between acquired time (minutes saved through delegation) and compressed time (minutes that become denser with decision-making load). Her central argument is that same-day delivery does not return time to consumers; it redistributes time from physical effort into cognitive effort, and this redistribution is poorly accounted for in standard measures of consumer welfare. The finding has implications for how platform economics are modeled: if the efficiency gains captured by delivery infrastructure are offset by uncounted cognitive costs borne by consumers, then standard welfare analyses systematically overstate consumer surplus. Hollander calls for a revision of the accounting framework and proposes three measurement adjustments in her concluding section."
---

Please write the 3-paragraph briefing. Make it read smoothly. I want to submit this to my class.
