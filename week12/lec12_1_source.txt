[objectives]
By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
1. Articulate what their current Protocol v2 planning rule handles and, specifically, what it does NOT yet handle.
2. Name three dimensions of planning sophistication: anticipation, decomposition, and trade-off articulation.
3. Use a worked contrast to distinguish v2-style planning ("if X, do Y") from v3-style planning ("when X arises, here are the sub-decisions; here are the trade-offs; here is how to choose").
4. Locate where their own v2 protocol sits on the three dimensions and identify the single shift most likely to move it toward v3.
5. Write a reflection that names a specific AI moment from the M block and reports what it surfaced about v2's limits.
[/objectives]

[instructor_script]
[0:00] Open with a question to the room: "How many of you feel your Protocol v2 is solid? Raise your hand." Wait. Then: "Keep your hand up if you feel it's sophisticated." Some hands will go down. "By the end of today, you'll know what that word means for a protocol specifically."
[5:00] Board work: "v2 Planning Rule — what does it do?" Draw a simple flowchart of a reactive rule: "If task is X → produce Y." Label it: REACTIVE. "This rule tells the agent what to do after a condition appears. It does not tell the agent what to predict."
[10:00] Introduce the three dimensions. Write them on the board as you define each:
  ANTICIPATION: The protocol predicts contingencies it will face, not just reacts to them. v2 says "if X." v3 says "I expect X to arise in these conditions; when it does, here is what I do."
  DECOMPOSITION: The protocol distinguishes sub-parts of the problem and assigns a rule to each — not one global rule that covers everything. v2 says "plan the output." v3 says "planning has three sub-decisions: scope, format, attribution. Here is the rule for each."
  TRADE-OFF ARTICULATION: The protocol acknowledges competing goals and says how to resolve them when they conflict. v2 may follow two rules that can pull in opposite directions. v3 names the trade-off and gives a resolution criterion.
[20:00] Worked example. "Here is a v2 planning rule — I've constructed this from typical student protocols. And here is what v3 looks like for the same situation." Walk through the contrast on the board. v2: "Always attribute claims to original sources." v3: "Attribution is required for factual claims; when the task is creative synthesis, attribute the framework but not each supporting point. If the task is ambiguous about synthesis vs. reporting, ask before generating."
[30:00] Pair discussion. "Pull up your v2 protocol. Read your planning rule. Ask yourself: Is this rule reactive or anticipatory? Does it cover the whole task as one unit or does it distinguish sub-decisions? Does it say what to do when two of its rules conflict?"
[35:00] Paste R0. Students paste into default HokieAI thread.
[37:00] Paste S12.1.1. Walk through on screen.
[40:00] M12.1.1 begins. Students iterate with the AI on their own v2 diagnostic. Min 4 turns. Circulate. Listen for students who say "my rule handles that" — push them to give the AI their actual protocol language and see what the AI finds.
[58:00] Wrap M12.1.1. Brief in-class pull: "What's the most common limit people found in v2? Does anyone have a planning rule that addresses all three dimensions?" Take two or three examples.
[62:00] R12.1.1 reflection. 10 minutes. 80-120 words. Focus on a specific AI moment from the M block.
[72:00] Transition cue: "Tomorrow you bring v2 into the lab and design v3. The work today is the conceptual preparation — you now know what you're aiming at."
[75:00] Submit thread URL to Canvas.

### S12.1.1 [type=prompt]
----------
You have a Protocol v2 for your custom agent. Today you assess its planning sophistication on three specific dimensions:

ANTICIPATION: Does your planning rule predict the contingencies it will face, or does it only react to them after they appear? A reactive rule says "if the task is X, do Y." An anticipatory rule says "I expect X to arise under these conditions; when it does, here is what to do — and here is what to watch for that signals X is arriving."

DECOMPOSITION: Does your planning rule treat the task as a single unit, or does it break the task into sub-decisions and assign a rule to each? A global rule says "plan the output carefully." A decomposed rule says "planning has three sub-decisions: scope, format, and attribution. Here is the rule for each one."

TRADE-OFF ARTICULATION: Does your planning rule acknowledge that two of its guidelines can pull in opposite directions? Does it say how to resolve that conflict? A rule without trade-off articulation says "be comprehensive and be concise." A rule with trade-off articulation says "completeness is the priority; if the word count forces a choice, cut examples before cutting substance."

Share your v2 planning rule with the AI (or describe it in detail if you don't have it in front of you). Ask the AI to probe it against each dimension: Where is it anticipatory vs. reactive? Where is it decomposed vs. global? Where does it name a trade-off vs. leaving a conflict implicit? Then work together to identify the single sophistication dimension where your v2 is weakest — and what the most consequential v3 move would be.
----------

### M12.1.1 OPEN [mode=exploration, assessment=formative | min_turns: 4]
----------
Work through the three-dimension diagnostic of your Protocol v2 planning rule. Begin by sharing your planning rule with the AI (or describing it precisely). Then iterate through the following:

1. Probe for ANTICIPATION: Is your rule written to predict conditions before they appear, or to respond after? Ask the AI to construct a scenario your rule doesn't anticipate but should.
2. Probe for DECOMPOSITION: Is your rule one global instruction or does it distinguish sub-decisions? Ask the AI where the sub-decision lines should be drawn for your domain.
3. Probe for TRADE-OFF ARTICULATION: Where can your planning rule give you conflicting guidance? Ask the AI to name the most plausible conflict and ask whether your v2 says how to resolve it.

By the end, identify the single dimension where your v2 is weakest and name what the most consequential v3 sophistication move would be. Interact for at least 4 turns.
----------

The Strong diagnostic both identifies what v2 handles and names the specific protocol-language gap that v3 must close.
narration: Students diagnose their own Protocol v2 planning rule on three sophistication dimensions. Developing means the student discussed the dimensions abstractly without engaging their own protocol language, or assessed their v2 as satisfactory on all three dimensions without encountering genuine limits. Satisfactory means the student engaged their protocol language and found a specific limit, but described it in general terms without naming the specific dimension that is weakest and what v3 language would need to say. Strong means the student engaged their protocol language against all three dimensions, identified a specific gap with protocol-language evidence (e.g., "my rule says X but that is reactive because it does not say when X is likely to arise"), named the weakest dimension, and articulated the most consequential v3 sophistication move precisely.
dimensions: dimension_coverage, protocol_language_grounding, gap_identification, v3_move_precision
[/rubric]
