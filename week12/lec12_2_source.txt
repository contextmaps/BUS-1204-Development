[objectives]
By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
1. Take their Protocol v2 and produce a written Protocol v3 by working the three planning dimensions from lec12_1 (anticipation, decomposition, trade-off articulation) into specific protocol language.
2. Locate the single clause in v2 where each of the three sophistication dimensions is weakest, and rewrite that clause to close the gap.
3. Distinguish v3 sophistication from longer protocol text — v3's anticipation clause is shorter than v2's reactive equivalent when written precisely.
4. Produce a Protocol v3 document that can be deployed to a custom agent in lec12_2a.
5. Write a reflection that names a specific AI moment from the M block and reports what the design work surfaced about the v2-to-v3 upgrade.
[/objectives]

### S12.2.1 [type=prompt]
----------
Today you upgrade your Protocol v2 to Protocol v3 by working the three planning dimensions from lec12_1 into specific protocol language.

v3 is not v2 with dimension labels appended. v3 is rewritten clauses in operational language — sentences your custom agent will actually run when it receives a task.

The discipline:
- ANTICIPATION goes into a clause that says what to flag and when. Not "be anticipatory." A v3 anticipation clause names a specific contingency, names the signal that the contingency is arriving, and says what to do when the signal appears.
- DECOMPOSITION goes into separate clauses for separate sub-decisions. Not "plan the output carefully." A v3 decomposition rule names the sub-decisions for a structured task and assigns one rule to each.
- TRADE-OFF ARTICULATION goes into a clause that names two competing guidelines and says which takes priority and when. Not "balance the trade-offs." A v3 trade-off clause names the conflict explicitly and gives a resolution criterion.

Bring your Protocol v2 into this exchange. Bring your lec12_1 diagnosis — which dimension is weakest in v2. With the AI, work through rewriting the clauses where the three dimensions are missing or weak. By the end of M12.2.1, you should have a written Protocol v3 ready to deploy to your custom agent in lec12_2a.
----------

### S12.2.2 [type=prompt, assessment=none]
----------
The synthesis scaffold for the M block. Use this to structure the exchange:

1. ANTICIPATION CLAUSE. Pull up the v2 clause closest to scope-or-task-framing. Ask the AI to probe it: which contingency in the kinds of tasks you give your agent is this clause written for, and which is it not written for? Draft a v3 anticipation clause that names a specific contingency and the signal that it is arriving. Read the v3 clause aloud — is it shorter and more specific than the v2 it replaces, or longer and vaguer?

2. DECOMPOSITION CLAUSES. Pull up the v2 planning rule. Identify a structured task your agent handles (a memo, a briefing, a comparison) and ask the AI: what are the sub-decisions inside that task? Draft v3 sub-decision rules — one per sub-decision. Read them aloud — do the rules have distinct content, or are they restatements of one global rule?

3. TRADE-OFF ARTICULATION CLAUSE. Identify two v2 guidelines that can pull in opposite directions for a realistic task. Ask the AI to name the conflict explicitly. Draft a v3 trade-off clause that names the competing guidelines and gives a resolution criterion (which takes priority and under what condition).

By the end, you should have at least one rewritten v3 clause for each dimension. Together with the unchanged parts of v2, these become your Protocol v3 document.

### M12.2.1 OPEN [mode=consolidation, assessment=summative | min_turns: 4]
----------
Design your Protocol v3. Paste your Protocol v2 clauses and your lec12_1 diagnosis (which dimension was weakest) into this exchange. With the AI, work through the synthesis scaffold from S12.2.2:

1. Probe and rewrite ANTICIPATION: identify the v2 clause closest to scope-or-task-framing, find the contingency it does not anticipate, and draft a v3 anticipation clause that names a specific contingency and the signal that it is arriving.
2. Probe and rewrite DECOMPOSITION: identify a structured task your agent handles, name the sub-decisions inside it, and draft a v3 decomposition rule — one clause per sub-decision.
3. Probe and rewrite TRADE-OFF ARTICULATION: identify two v2 guidelines that can pull in opposite directions, name the conflict, and draft a v3 clause with an explicit resolution criterion.

By the end of M12.2.1, produce a Protocol v3 document — the rewritten clauses integrated with the parts of v2 that did not need changing. That document is what you will deploy to your custom agent in lec12_2a. Interact for at least 4 turns.

Strong means the student engaged v2 language for all three dimensions, drafted v3 clauses with specific operational content (named contingency for anticipation, named sub-decisions for decomposition, named conflict and resolution criterion for trade-off articulation), and produced a v3 document with concrete clause-level evidence for each rewrite.

[rubric id: R12.2.1]
type: reflection
context_prompt: S12.2.1
directive: In 100-140 words, reflect on what the design exchange in M12.2.1 surfaced about the v2-to-v3 upgrade. The reflection must name at least one specific moment from the AI conversation in the M block — a phrase the AI used, a probe the AI made, a v2 limit the AI pointed out, or a question that exposed a clause needing a rewrite. Report what that moment surfaced. Close with which of the three sophistication dimensions most changed your protocol and what v3 now says that v2 did not.
narration: Reflection on the v3 design exchange. Developing means the student summarizes the three dimensions or describes the upgrade in general terms without citing a specific moment from the AI conversation in the M block — the threshold rule applies (no AI-interaction-moment citation caps at Developing regardless of writing quality). Satisfactory means the student cites a specific AI moment from the M block and reports what it surfaced about the upgrade — but the reflection stays within the frame of what the AI's content produced rather than developing independent conclusions or forward-looking implications. The four-layer signature: (1) specific AI-interaction-moment cited, (2) third-person reportorial voice throughout ("the AI noted...", "the discussion surfaced...", "it became clear that..."), (3) no causal-attribution sentences linking moments to outcomes ("X determined Y", "the session identified Z as the variable"), (4) comparative summary statements attributed to the AI explicitly ("the AI noted that anticipation changed more than decomposition", not the freestanding "anticipation changed more than decomposition"). Strong means the student cites a specific AI moment, reports what it surfaced, and extends the analysis independently — an insight or implication the AI did not name that the student arrived at through their own reasoning. First-person processing language ("I see how...", "I realized...", "this makes me want to..."), causal attribution, or freestanding comparative analysis bumps a within-AI-frame reflection to Strong.
dimensions: ai_moment_specificity, upgrade_articulation, dimension_change_identification, independent_extension
[/rubric]
