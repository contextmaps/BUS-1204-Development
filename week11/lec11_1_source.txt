[objectives]
By the end of this lecture, students will be able to:
1. Articulate in plain language the difference between a large language model and an agent.
2. Name what an agent adds to an underlying language model: instructions, defined role, knowledge sources, and optional tools.
3. Use their own custom agent (Study Helper or equivalent) as a concrete example of the LLM-vs-agent distinction.
4. Explain the "wrapping" concept in terms a classmate who missed Week 8 could understand.
5. Recognize that they have been designing agents since Week 8 without having had a name for what they were doing.
[/objectives]

[context actor=student]
Since Week 8 you have been building and testing a custom agent. You configured its instructions, tested its behavior, observed where it differed from a fresh prompt. Today the course names what you have been doing. An agent is not a different kind of AI — it is an LLM with instructions, a role, and optional knowledge sources wrapped around it. The underlying model is the same one you used in Week 1. What changed is what was wrapped around it.
[/context]

### S11.1.1 [type=prompt]
----------
In Week 1, you sent prompts to an AI and read its responses. In Week 8, you configured a custom agent — Study Helper, or whatever you named it — and ran tasks through it.

What changed? The underlying language model is the same. But something around it changed.

In your own words, describe the difference. What is an LLM? What is an agent? What does it mean that you can wrap one inside the other? Use your own course experience as the grounding — the prompts you sent in Week 1, the agent you built in Week 8, the way your agent behaves now compared to how a fresh prompt would behave.

Then ask the AI to help you tighten your articulation. Get to a clean explanation you could use to teach this distinction to a classmate who missed Week 8.
----------
###

What did Study Helper know that a fresh prompt would not? After your first attempt, ask the AI where your articulation is still vague or imprecise. Keep iterating until you have a clean explanation you could actually say out loud to a classmate.
[/context]
